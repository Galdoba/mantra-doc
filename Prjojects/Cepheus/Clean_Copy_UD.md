# SYSTEM GENERATION

If you already have a subsector map to explore, you may skip ahead to step  8. If not, begin with step 1. Roll d100 on the chart in step 1 and this  will determine if there is an object present in that hex. Roll once for  each hex in the subsector.

## Determine the presence of an object in the hex

| Subsector Type | Roll       |
|----------------|------------|
| Empty          | 05 or less |
| Scattered      | 20 or less |
| Dispersed      | 35 or less |
| Average        | 50 or less |
| Crowded        | 60 or less |
| Dense          | 75 or less |

If an object is present, proceed to step 2. Roll d100 on the chart to  determine what sort of object is present in the hex.

## 02 Determine the type of object.

| D100  | Object Type     |
|-------|-----------------|
| 01-80 | Star            |
| 81-88 | Brown Dwarf     |
| 89-94 | Rogue Planet    |
| 95-97 | Rogue Gas Giant |
| 98    | Neutron Star    |
| 99    | Nebula          |
| 00    | Black Hole      |

If the object is a star, proceed to step 4. If the result is a Brown  Dwarf, continue to step 3. If the result is a rogue planet, proceed to  step 15 (p.83). If the result is a rogue gas giant, proceed to step 13  (p.75). If the result is a neutron star or nebula, then proceed to step 18  to place the Zimm Points. If it is a Black Hole, then the process is  finished for this hex. A Black Hole will have no Zimm Points.

## 05 Roll 1d10 to determine the numerical classification between 0 and 9.

Roll 1d10 to get the numerical classification of the star or brown dwarf.  Each stellar class breaks down into 10 types numbered from 0 to 9. 0 is  the warmest while 9 is the coolest. If this object is a star, proceed to  step 6. If this is a brown dwarf, go to step 7.

## 06 Roll on the following chart to determine the Luminosity Class

Each star belongs to a luminosity class which determines the brightness of  the star. This is often referred to as the MKK system from the initials of  the last names of the scientists who developed the classification system  (William Morgan, Phillip Keenan, and Edith Kellman) in 1943.

| D100  | Luminosity Class        |
|-------|-------------------------|
| 01-90 | V (Main Sequence)       |
| 91-94 | IV (Subgiants)          |
| 95-96 | D (Dwarf)               |
| 97-99 | III (Giant)             |
| 00    | Roll on the chart below |

| D10 | Luminosity Class         |
|-----|--------------------------|
| 1-4 | II (Bright Giant)        |
| 5   | VI (Subdwarf)            |
| 7-8 | Ia (Luminous Supergiant) |
| 9-0 | Ib (Supergiant)          |

If the star is determined to be in Luminosity Class “D” then it is listed  as “D” and does not have a stellar class. Roll 1d10 to determine the type  digit.

For stars which are O, B, A, or F, replace any luminosity of VI with V.

## 09 Determine the number of gas giants

If the primary object in the system is a star, roll 1d6-2 to determine the  number of gas giants in the system. If the primary object is an L-class  Brown Dwarf, roll 1d6-4. If the primary object is a T-class Brown Dwarf,  roll 1d6-5. If it is a Y-class Brown Dwarf, there will be no gas giant in  the system. If this is a multiple star system, feel free to distribute the  gas giants among the stars as you see fit. Remember that stars with  companions may have limited space available for orbiting planets. Treat  any result of less than zero as 0.

## 10 Determine the number of asteroid belts

Roll 1d6-3. This is the number of asteroid belts in the system. If this is  a multiple star system, feel free to distribute the asteroid belts among  the stars as you see fit. Remember that stars with companions may have  limited space available for asteroid belts. Treat any result of less than  zero as 0.

## 11 Determine the habitable zone of the star

Worlds which are habitable will be located within the habitable zone.  Worlds which have already been determined to be garden worlds should be  located in the center of the habitable zone. Closer to the inner edge of  the habitable zone will result in warmer worlds whereas placing the world  in the outer areas of the habitable zone will produce cooler worlds. More  will be determined concerning planetary temperature in section concerning  climate on p. 136-144.

Temperature of the star is given in Kelvin. Mass is given in solar masses  (or “standard” in Clement Sector terminology). Luminosity is given in  solar units (or “standard” in Clement Sector terms).

The inner limit is the closest to the star that a planet may be placed.  The outer limit is the furthest from the star that a planet may be placed.  In addition, multiple star systems may have other restrictions (see Step  8). Distances are given in astronomical units.

The habitable zone is the range in which a world is likely to be able to  support life. Please note that this is not always the case. Some systems  may have worlds within the habitable zone which are not capable of  supporting life while other systems may have multiple worlds within the  zone which are capable of doing so. Some stars will have their habitable  zone listed as N/A or non-applicable which means that the star’s  theoretical habitable zone would be further out than the star’s outer  limit. The distances are given in astronomical units.

The snow line is the distance from the star where it was cold enough  during planet formation for water ice to stay frozen. Some stars will have  their snow line listed as N/A or non-applicable. This means that planets  which have formed inside the outer limit will never be cold enough to have  formed this solid.

When dealing with a multiple star system, the zones may need to be  adjusted. In general, if the binary pair are in contact, simply add the  zones of the two stars together to get the result. For instance, if the  primary star has an inner limit of 0.6 AU and the contact companion has an  inner limit of 0.7 AU, then the inner limit for the pair will be 1.3 AU.

For other companions, the inner limit will be the same for each star. The  outer limit for planets orbiting one of the two companions will be 0.3  times the distance between the two stars unless the outer limit for the  star is less than this.

Some planets will orbit both stars of a close companion. In those cases,  the inner limit of both stars will be 2.5 times the distance between the  two stars.

Generally speaking, if you are creating a realistic system, O type stars  will not have planets or gas giants but may have asteroid belts. On the  other hand, if your system is intended to be less realistic, you may wish  to place worlds around these stars. Zones have been provided to assist you  in this.

Main sequence stars are listed first in the charts as they are more  common. Following this, the star types are listed in the order of Ia, Ib,  II, III, and VI. Zones have also been provided for brown dwarfs. As you  will see from the chart, habitable worlds around T and Y class brown  dwarfs are not going to be possible in a realistic system. However, if you  don’t mind something a bit less realistic, we’ve provided the possible  orbiting zones as well.

## 12. Main World Placement

If you already have a created world or have a good idea where you believe  the world should be placed, go ahead and place the world where you believe  it should be. You may have to modify this somewhat as the process  continues but it may be advantageous to you to know where you want the  planet to orbit.

If you don’t have a need to place a world, just ignore this step and move  on to step 13.

## 13. Gas Giants

Gas giants are the largest planets of a solar system. Where they orbit and  where they formed will often have a large effect on where you will be able  to place other objects in the system. As such, unless you already know  that you need to have a planet in a certain location, it is best to start  with the gas giants.

If you have no gas giants in the system, simply skip to step 14.

Roll to determine the size of the gas giant. Roll 1d6. On a result of 1-3,  you have a small gas giant, also called a Neptunian as it will be near the  size of Neptune. On a roll of 4-6, you have a large gas giant, also called  a Jovian as it will be near the size of Jupiter.

You should place the gas giants at or beyond the snow line. If there is no  snow line listed, the snow line is beyond where a planet could form and  thus there are no gas giants in this system. Remove the gas giants and  continue generating the remainder of the system.

The larger gas giants should be placed nearer to the snow line with the  smaller gas giants being placed further out in the system. Don’t determine  their orbits yet but have a good idea of the order in which the planets  will be placed.

If you have already determined that there will need to be a rocky planet  or a main world in the inner system of the solar system, skip ahead to  step 14. If not, proceed to the next paragraph.

You will need to determine if a gas giant has migrated. Use the largest  gas giant in your system. If the gas giants are all the same size, then  you should use the gas giant closest to the snow line. Roll 1d6. If the  result is a “6”, then the gas giant has migrated inward.

Roll d100. This is the number of Astronomical Units (AUs) that the gas  giant has migrated inward. If the number is higher than the number of  astronomical units that the gas giant originally orbited, then  automatically move the gas giant to the Inner Limit listed for the star. Any planet  which you might have placed that was in the system between your migrating  gas giant and the star has now been thrown from the system. There will be  no planets formed between the original location of the gas giant and the  star.

If you have more gas giants in the system that have not migrated, place  the orbit of the second innermost gas giant somewhere beyond the snow  line.

## 14. Orbital Placement

Building from either the gas giant near the snow line, the migrated gas  giant near the star, or the main world which you generated before you  started this process, we will now place other worlds into orbit around the  star. If you do not have a planet in the system at this point, generate a  main world using the rules in the Clement Sector: The Rules (p.174).

To determine how many rocky planets that there are in your solar system,  roll $1d6+1$. This is the number of rocky worlds which are in your star  system. Write this number down.

Now that you have this number of planets in your system, it is time to  place them in orbits. There are two schools of thought concerning  placement of planets and we will cover both. Feel free to use either  method.

### First School - Doubling

In the past, many people have noted that the distances in AU from the sun  of planets in our own solar system is roughly double as you proceed from  the sun. After a while, however, this system begins to break down and the  theory has largely been discredited in the scientific community. However,  for our purposes, one can easily see how this could be used to simulate a  working star system.

Start with the gas giant which you placed near the snow line. Assuming  that it has not migrated inward, you may use this placement as a baseline.  First, let’s place the rocky planets and asteroid belts. Divide the  distance in half and place your first world roughly there. Repeat the  process until you have placed all of your planets and asteroid belts or  you have reached the inner limit.

If you need to round the distances, feel free to do so. You can round them  using standard rounding methods (which is highly recommended), always  round up, or always round down. It’s your choice. It’s your star system.

However, once you get a number below the inner limit, you can either  discard that planet or you can place it elsewhere. You could choose to  place your rocky planet into the outer system beyond the snow line or even  as a dwarf planet in an asteroid belt (such as Ceres in our own solar  system). When placing planets of any kind or asteroid belts going toward  the outer limit, simply double the distance in AUs for the next placement.

There are no hard rules on placing asteroid belts. It is recommended that  you place them between your rocky planets and your gas giant but there is  no reason that the belt or belts couldn’t be nearer to the star than that  or further out beyond your gas giants. It is also possible that a belt  could be in an orbit between your gas giants.

If you don’t have a gas giant in the system, you can choose one of the  other worlds and use that as your baseline. This is particularly useful if  you already know that you require a habitable main world and you’ve placed  it in the center of the habitable zone. You can then work from the  habitable main world as your baseline.

For instance, Jenna is creating a system with an F6 IV star and placed her  largest gas giant near the snow line at 20.50 AU from the star. She has  five planets and one asteroid belt to place. Feeling traditional, Jenna  decides to place her asteroid belt between the gas giant and her rocky  planets. She has the asteroid belt placed at a distance of 10.25 AU.

For her first rocky body placement, she puts the planet at 5.13 AU  (rounding up). This is inside the habitable zone, so that could be  interesting later. For the second planet placement, Jenna puts it at 2.57  AU. Too warm for the habitable zone. For the third planet, she places it  at 1.29 AU. For the fourth, she places it at 0.65 AU. That would place the  fifth planet at 0.33 AU. However, this is inside the inner limit of 0.56  AU. Looks like Jenna is going to lose that fifth planet.

Now it is time to place the gas giants. Again, you will use the gas giant  which you placed near the snow line as your base. Double that distance and  place the next gas giant there. Continue this process until you have an  orbit which goes beyond the outer limit or you’ve run out of gas giants.

Continuing our example, Jenna is now going to work on her gas giants. She  has four to place. Using the gas giant she placed near the snow line at  20.50 AU, she is going to double that and place her next gas giant at 41  AU. Her third gas giant would orbit at 82 AU. Her fourth would then be  placed at 164 AU. However, the outer limit for an F6 IV star is 112 AU.  Jenna will lose the fourth gas giant but she has completed her star  system.

Remember that doubling is a largely discredited theory at this point, this  will not make a scientifically accurate system. However, it will create a  sufficiently realistic system for play in a “Fantastic” or “S “Semi-realistic” campaign.

### Second School – Varied Distance

A more realistic method of placing planets would use a similar method as  doubling but, instead of multiplying or dividing by two, would require  varying the factor used to multiply or divide the distance. This can be  achieved by rolling a d10 with the result filling in the “X” in the number  1.X. If a “0” is rolled, change the factor to 2.0 rather than 1.X.

For instance, Sherri is creating a star system and wants a more realistic  system. She begins by placing the rocky bodies just as Jenna did in the  previous example. Sherri rolls a d10 and gets a 4. This gives her a result  of 1.4 to use as the divider for the planet in the next orbit inward. If  she had rolled a 6, she would divide the distance by 1.6. If she had  rolled a 0, she would divide by 2.

After placing the gas giant near the snow line, you would begin placing  rocky planets and asteroid belts into their orbits as you move toward the  star. Rolling for each world, divide the distance by the result of 1.X.  Repeat the process until you have placed all of your planets and asteroid  belts or you have reached the inner limit. If you need to round the  results, do so using the standard method.

However, once you get a number below the inner limit, you should then  discard that planet. You’ve run out of room.

There are no hard rules on placing asteroid belts. It is recommended that  you place them between your rocky planets and your gas giant but there is  no reason that the belt or belts couldn’t be nearer to the star than that.

If you don’t have a gas giant in the system, you can choose one of the  other worlds and use that as your baseline. This is particularly useful if  you already know that you require a habitable main world and you’ve placed  it in the center of the habitable zone. You can then work from the  habitable main world as your baseline.

Sherri is creating a system with a GO V star. She has four planets to  place and two asteroid belts. She has no large gas giants but has a small  gas giant placed at 6.75 AU near the snow line. She rolls a d10 and gets a  3. She divides 6.75 by 1.3 and gets 5.1923076923. She rounds this to 5.19  and places her asteroid belt here.

For the next orbit, she rolls a d10 and gets 5. She divides 5.19 by 1.5  and gets a result of 3.46 and decides to place her second asteroid belt  here.

For the next orbit, Sherri rolls a d10 and gets 2. She divides 3.46 by 1.2  and gets a result of 2.88333333 and rounds this to 2.88. She places her  first of four rocky planets here.

For the next orbit, Sherri rolls the d10 and gets 0. So she divides 2.88  by 2 and gets a result of 1.44. This is near the center of the habitable  zone and may prove interesting later!

For her third planet placement, she rolls a d10 and gets a 7. So Sherri  divides 1.44 by 1.7 and gets a result of 0.8470588235. She rounds this to  0.85 and places the third of her rocky planets here.

Rolling for the last of her rocky worlds, Sherri gets an 8. She divides  0.85 by 1.8 and gets a result of 0.47222222222. She rounds this to 0.47.  As this is outside of the star’s inner limit, she is able to place her  fourth and final rocky planet in this orbit.

For any remaining gas giants, you repeat the procedure except now you will  multiply by the result of entering the result of the d10 into the X in  1.X.

Sherri has two other gas giants to place. Again, starting at the gas giant  at 6.75 AU as a baseline, she rolls a d10. She gets a result of 3. She  multiplies 6.75 by 1.3 and then places the first of these two gas giants  at 8.78 AU.

For the final gas giant, she rolls a 0. She multiplies 8.78 by 2 and gets  a result of 17.56. This is well within the star’s outer limit of 44 AU, so  she places this final gas giant in orbit at 17.56 AU

## 18. Placing Zimm Points

If you are using the Clement Sector setting, then you will need to place  the Incoming and Outgoing Zimm Points. There will be one Incoming Zimm  Point (IZP) and one Outgoing Zimm Point (OZP) for each system within two  parsecs (or hexes) on the subsector map. If you do not yet know what is in  the neighboring systems, then wait until you have populated the entire  subsector before continuing.

Hexes with rogue planets, rogue gas giants, neutron stars, or nebulas will  also have Zimm Points. You may place the Zimm Points for these at any  location beyond 10 AU from the planet, neutron star, or the center of the  nebula.

Place the Incoming Zimm Points first. Look at the subsector map and jot  down each system within two hexes. We recommend starting at the hexes  above the system which you are creating and then working

your way clockwise until you are finished. Each of those systems will need  to have an IZP within the system that you are creating.

Incoming Zimm Points should be placed in the inner system first and then  spread to the outer system as necessary. Zimm Points should be at a  minimum distance of 0.25 AU from other objects and Zimm Points in the  system. Zimm Points must be located between the Inner Limit and the Outer  Limit of the system.

Once the IZPs are placed, the Outgoing Zimm Points (OZPs) must be placed.  Ideally, these should be placed beyond the point of the outermost IZP. The  OZPs should be at a minimum distance of 0.25 AU from other objects and one  another.

## 19. Moons

Moons are defined by the Cascadia Colonization Authority to be any  naturally occurring object of larger than 10km in diameter that orbits a  planet. To determine if a planet has moons, use the following chart.

| Size of Planet  | \# of Moons |
|-----------------|-------------|

| 1-3             | 1d6-4       |
| 4-6             | 1d6-3       |
| 7-Q             | 1d6-2       |
| Small Gas Giant | 2d6-4       |
| Large Gas Giant | 2d6+4       |

If the planet is size 5 or less, roll 1d6 to determine the size of the  moon. On an even number, the size is 0. On an odd result, the size is 1.

If the planet is sizes 6-8, roll 1d6-3 to determine the size of the moon.  Treat any result of less than zero as size 0.

If the planet is sizes 10 (A) – 15 (F), roll 1d6-2 to determine the size  of the moon. Treat any result of less than zero as size 0.

If the planet is size 16 (G) – 24 (Q), roll 1d6-1 to determine the size of  the moon.

If the planet is a small gas giant, roll 1d6 to determine the size of the  moon.

If the planet is a large gas giant, roll 1d6+1 to determine the size of  the moon.

For gas giants, there is a possibility that there will be a wide  assortment of moonlets which will also be in orbit around the planet. If  you wish to determine how many of these moonlets are in orbit, roll d100.

Orbits for moons are generally thought of as being within orbits  determined by multiples of the diameter of the planet. For instance, if a  planet has a diameter of 5000 km then something orbiting at 2 diameters  would orbit at a distance of 10,000 km from the planet, something at 3  diameters would orbit at 15,000 km and so forth.

Rocky planets will have their moons orbit at a distance of 4-40 diameters.  While the best method is to simply choose the orbit in which you place  your worlds, if you wish to determine this randomly roll 2d10. Add the  results on the dice together and multiply it by two.

Gas giants will generally have their small moonlets orbiting within the  first five diameters and then again at a larger distance of 30 diameters.  While you will likely not want to determine the orbits of all of a gas  giant’s moonlets, it might become necessary to do so during the course of  a game. With moonlets, it is best to choose where the moonlet orbits  within the above parameters.

For larger moons orbiting a gas giant, either choose the orbits from the  above parameters or roll $1d6 \times 5$ . For further refinement, roll a  $d10$ and assign this to fill any zero in the result. For example, you  roll a 4 on the d6. This gives an orbit of 20 diameters. You can then  refine that by rolling a d10. Rolling a d10, you get a 7. You may then say  that the moon is orbiting at 27 diameters. You can further refine this by  rolling a d10 again. Rolling a d10, you get a 4. You may then say that the  moon is orbiting at 27.4 diameters.

# SYSTEM CREATION EXAMPLES

## System 1

Larry wishes to create a system for a Clement Sector game that he will be  running at his local game shop. He sits down to create a system in which  to base the adventures. He has no idea what sort of system he wants so he  decides to let the dice do the talking.

He knows that he wants a star system and not a rogue planet, brown dwarf,  or black hole, so he skips to the table which gives him a star type. Larry  decides that he wants a realistic system so he rolls on the realistic  chart. He rolls d100 and gets an 85. His star will be “K” class.

Moving to step 5, Larry rolls a d10 and gets a 5. Then he moves to step 6,  rolls d100 and gets a 45. 45 is a “V” or main sequence star. Larry’s star  will be a K5 V.

Larry rolls to see if his system is a single star system in step 7. He  rolls d100 and gets a 23. It is a single star system.

He rolls to determine if there are any gas giants in the system as per  step 9. Larry rolls a 1, so there are no gas giants in the system. Moving  on to step 10, Larry rolls a 3. This gives a result of 0 and, thus, there  are no asteroid belts in the system.

Moving on to step 11, Larry finds the zones which correspond with the K5 V  star that he rolled. A K5 V star has an inner limit of 0.14 AU, a  habitable zone of 0.49-0.68 AU, a snow line of 2.60 AU, and an outer limit  of 28 AU.

Larry does not have a pre-existing main world so he moves past step 12. He  has no gas giants, so moves past step 13.

Arriving at step 14, Larry rolls to determine how many rocky planets will  be in his system. He rolls 1d6+1 and gets a result of 7. Larry decides  that since he has no existing worlds in this system yet, he will create a  main world and have that be one of the 7 rocky worlds. He uses the rules  in Clement Sector: The Rules (p.174) to create a world with a UWP of  A684555-A. As this appears to be a habitable world, he places it in the  habitable zone. He decides to place it at 0.60 AU.

Now he has six more worlds to place. Larry decides to use the doubling  system and places a rocky planet at 0.30 AU and another at 0.15 AU. As the  inner limit for this star is 0.14 AU, he must place the remaining four  worlds outside of his main world. Larry places rocky planets at 1.20 AU,  2.40 AU, 4.80 AU, and 9.60 AU.

Tackling the innermost worlds first, Larry rolls for the type of the  planet at 0.15 AU. He rolls 2d6 and gets a 6. The first world will be a  Mercurian. He rolls for the second world at 0.30 AU and gets a 7. This  world, too, will be a Mercurian.

Taking the first world at 0.15, Larry rolls for size (as on p.86). He  rolls a d6 and gets a 5. As this is an odd number, the size will be 3.  Rolling 1d6-3 for the atmosphere, Larry gets a result of 6. The atmosphere  will be code 3. Hydrographics code is 0.

## Mass

The mass of the planet can be determined by using this formula. The  formula will give you the mass of the planet in Earth Masses or “s “standard”.

$$ M = K \times (R/8)^3 $$

- $M$ = Mass - $K$ = Density in Earth Densities - $R$ = Planet’s Size Code

If the planet’s size code is 0, use 0.5 instead.

# Surface Gravity

The surface gravity of the planet can be determined using the following  formula. The formula will give you the surface gravity of the planet in  Earth Gravities or “standard”.

$$ G = M \times (64/R^2) $$

- $G$ = Surface Gravity in Earth Gravities - $M$ = Planet’s Mass in Earth Masses - $R$ = Size Code for the Planet     If the planet’s size code is 0, use 0.5 instead.

---

# Orbital Period

The orbital period for a planet is found using the formula listed below  this paragraph.

$$ P = \sqrt{D^3 / M} $$

- $P$ = Orbital Period in standard years. - $D$ = Distance from the star in Astronomical Units (AU) - $M$ = Mass of the central star

The result of this formula will give the amount of time that it takes for  the planet to perform a complete orbit around its star. This result will  be given in Earth or “standard” years. If you wish to know how many  Standard Days it takes for the planet to orbit the star, simply multiply  the result by 365.25.

---

# Rotation Period

To find the rotation period of your planet, you will need to use the  formula listed below this paragraph. Roll $4d10+5$ and use the result of  that as $W$ in the equation.

$$ P = W + M/D $$

- $P$ = Rotation period in hours - $W = 4d10 + 5$ - $M$ = Mass of the star being orbited - $D$ = The orbital distance from the planet to the star in Astronomical  Units (AU)

# ATMOSPHERE DETAILS

## Atmospheric Composition

For an atmosphere code of 1, the atmosphere is no more than a trace.  Characters arriving on the planet will have to wear vacc suits to travel  on the planet. The composition of such atmospheres is often unbreathable  even at higher pressures but with an atmospheric pressure this low it will  hardly matter to visitors to the planet.

For atmospheres with a code of 2-9, the atmosphere is considered to be a  nitrogen-oxygen mixture similar to that of Earth with the main issue being  that of pressure and taint. The standard nitrogen-oxygen mixture is 78.08%  nitrogen, 20.95% oxygen, 0.93% argon, 0.04% carbon dioxide.

Atmospheres with codes 2,4,7, and 9 have a taint to that nitrogen-oxygen  mixture. These atmospheres contain some element that is harmful to humans,  such as an unusually high proportion of carbon dioxide. A character who  breathes a tainted atmosphere without a filter of some kind will suffer  1D6 damage every few minutes (or hours, depending on the level of taint).  You may choose what this taint is or roll on the chart below this  paragraph.

An atmosphere with the code of 10 (A) will also have these same taints but  will have them as a far larger percentage of the overall atmosphere than  what one would find in a 2,4,7, or 9 atmosphere. For example, sulfur as a  taint in a 2,4,7,or 9 atmosphere might reach as high as 1% of the total  atmosphere while on a planet with a 10 (A) atmosphere, the sulfur could  reach 5-10% or even replace the usual 20% oxygen with 20% sulfur dioxide.

The taint of an atmosphere may tell you interesting things concerning the  world itself. Worlds with a large amount of pollen or spores in the  atmosphere means that there will be a large amount of plant life on the  planet.

Worlds with a chlorine or fluorine taint will have an atmosphere that can  be toxic to humans without filter masks or even protective suits. While  worlds with a 2,4,7, or 9 atmosphere will have tiny amounts of chlorine or  fluorine in the atmosphere (certainly less than 0.1%), the heavier  chemicals may settle into caves or lower altitudes causing areas with  little to no native life. On the other hand, life may evolve to

live in that environment and not in the overall atmosphere. In any case,  chlorine and fluorine are extremely dangerous and would be deadly to  humans even in small amounts in the atmosphere.

Atmospheres with a sulfur taint would indicate volcanic activity as would  those atmospheres with volcanic ash as the taint. Sulfuric gases in the  atmosphere may create acidic rain which can be quite harmful to humans.  Volcanic ash may not only be dangerous to humans without a filter mask but  may also damage the filter mask itself requiring replacement filters far  faster than other taints. Volcanic ash can kill humans by settling in the  lungs.

Dust, too, can settle in the lungs and cause death for a human. Dust in  the atmosphere may also indicate a lack of plant life in the area as the  loose soil or sand is carried by the wind.

High amounts of oxygen in the atmosphere will increase the chances of  fires starting and can also be cause damage to humans without filter  masks. Higher amounts of oxygen may cause skin problems as well. Lightning  storms in such an environment would almost certainly start fires that may  spread quickly. Low amounts of oxygen can make it difficult for humans to  operate without filter masks which provide an adequate amount. Lower  oxygen levels can make it seem like you are traveling at a higher  elevation and can cause increased fatigue.

Biotoxins in the atmosphere may indicate a plant or animal which secretes  such a toxin. These may be mild irritants or full-fledged poison  atmospheres which have an effect like the venom on an animal. See Clement  Sector: The Rules (p.171) for more on poisons.

Disease in the atmosphere may be any kind of airborne disease which may be  deadly or simply an irritant to the breather. See Clement Sector: The  Rules (p.170) for more on disease.

Nitrogen oxides may cause an unprotected breather to act as if they are  intoxicated. While it may not kill a person in the sorts of low  concentrations which constitute a taint, they may cause a human to lose  dexterity or have lapses in judgement.

An atmosphere with code 11 (B) is considered to be a corrosive atmosphere.  This is any atmosphere which is classified as being one which can be  survived using a vacc suit and will not break down the vacc suit in a  short amount of time. A broad category to be sure. The CCA generally uses  code 11 (B) to indicate any atmosphere with a mixture that is not a  variant of the nitrogen-oxygen mix and will not kill landing parties by  defeating their suits within a matter of minutes.

While the CCA uses this code as a catch-all for any non-invasive  atmosphere, there are a number of recurring atmospheric types which are  commonly encountered.

# Greenhouse Effect

To determine the Greenhouse Effect on your world, solve the formula listed  below this paragraph.

$$ E = (P/6) \times (P/G) $$

- $E$ = Greenhouse Effect - $P$ = Atmospheric Pressure in Standard - $G$ = Surface Gravity of the Planet in Standard

# Average Temperature

To determine the average temperature on your planet, you must first  determine the Effective Temperature or Blackbody Temperature. To do this,  you must solve the equation listed below this paragraph.

$$ B = (271 \times \sqrt[4]{L}) / \sqrt{D} $$

- $B$ = Blackbody Temperature in Kelvin - $L$ = Solar Luminosity of the Central Star in Solar Units - $D$ = Distance of the planet from the stat in Astronomical Units

Once this has been determined, the Average Temperature can be found by  using the equation listed below this paragraph.

$$ T = B \times [\sqrt[4]{(1 - A)}] \times (1 + E) $$

- $T$ = Average Surface Temperature in Kelvin - $B$ = Blackbody Temperature in Kelvin - $A$ = Albedo - $E$ = Greenhouse Effect

This temperature can be converted to Celsius by subtracting 273.15 from  the Kelvin result. If you wish to convert this temperature to Fahrenheit,  multiply the Kelvin result by 1.8 and then subtract 459.67.

The average temperature of the planet is affected by the world’s orbital  eccentricity. Use the orbital eccentricity which you determined on p.82.  To determine the effect on temperature at the closest approach to the  star, multiply the eccentricity by 30. Add this to the average temperature  for the period where the planet is closest to star and subtract this from  the average temperature for the period where the planet is furthest from  the star.

The average temperature is the temperature which can be commonly found in  the temperate regions during temperate seasons (such as Spring and Fall on  Earth). As an average, the temperature experienced by the characters in  these regions will vary by up to 10 degrees Celsius according to  conditions.

To determine the temperature faced on the planet by those who reside or  visit there, first convert the average temperature to Celsius. From there,  you can determine the equatorial temperature and the polar temperature.

Equatorial temperatures will generally be higher than the average and  unaffected by seasons on most worlds. On worlds with atmospheres of higher  than a code of 2, equatorial temperatures will increase by a number equal  to the size code of the planet multiplied by three. If the axial tilt is  lower than 35 degrees, use the following formula.

$$ Q = (3 \times S) + T $$

- $ Q $ = Equatorial Temperature (in Celsius) - $ S $ = Size Code of the Planet - $ T $ = Average Temperature (in Celsius)

If the axial tilt of the planet is higher than 35 degrees, then you will  need to take the axial tilt into account to get an accurate equatorial  temperature. If the tilt is higher than 35 degrees, consult the chart  below to determine a multiplier.

# HYDROSPHERE DETAILS

The hydrosphere of a planet is represented by the fourth digit in the UWP  (see Clement Sector: The Rules, p.177). This digit is a representation of  the percentage of water to found on the planet. A “4” in the hydrosphere  digit means that somewhere between 40% and 49% of the planet is covered in  water. If you wish to narrow this a bit, you may roll a d10 to determine  the exact percentage. For instance, if the hydrosphere is 40% and you roll  a d10 and get “6”, then your planet has 46% coverage in water. Rolling a � “0” on your d10, in this case, will equal a “0”.

Standing water will only be possible on subterran, terran, and superterran  worlds located within the habitable zone (see p.92-98). Worlds with a  tainted atmosphere will also have this taint present in the hydrosphere  and, according to the taint, can make it dangerous for humans, altrants,  or uplifts to drink. For worlds in the outer regions of the habitable zone  and those rare worlds (see Quirks, p.117) beyond the snow line which have  water on them, this water will take the form of ice rather than standing  water.

# OTHER FACTORS

## Multiple Stars

Some systems have more than one star that might affect the average  temperature of the planet. If the stars are in contact or are close  companions, then simply add the two star’s luminosities and complete the  process (as seen on p.49) as normal. While it does not always work out, a  general rule of thumb is that you can also add the distances of the  habitable zones together to get a rough estimate of where the new  habitable zone will be. Experimentation in cases like this will often be  required of the person building a system like this.

For distant companion stars, the luminosity will be negligible and can be  ignored.

In the case of close, near, and far companion stars, you will have to  determine how much of the other star or stars’ luminosity reaches the  planet. To do this, you will have to use the formula listed below this  paragraph.

$$ Y = L \div \sqrt{(C \div W) \div 2} $$

- $Y$ = Amount of Luminosity to be added to the primary star’s luminosity - $L$ = Luminosity of the companion star - $C$ = Distance of the companion star in Astronomical Units (AUs) - $W$ = Distance of the planet from the primary star in Astronomical Units  (AUs)

Simply add the result ( $Y$ ) to the luminosity of the primary star and  complete the process (as seen on p.49) as usual

# Biology
